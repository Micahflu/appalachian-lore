If you travel to East Second Street in Chillicothe, Ohio you’ll see a black arch with a lit sign that reads “Majestic”. You’ll see next to it a building with stained glass windows and a marquee advertising its latest events. This 160-year-old building has watched time pass through Chillicothe and was even involved in one of the city’s most tragic events. This building is the Majestic Theatre.

In one of Patricia Fife Medert’s books titled “Second Street Chillicothe, Ohio Its Buildings & Its People”, you’ll find a detailed description of the history of the building on East Second Street. In January of 1809 the newly built building began as the Bank of Chillicothe. Throughout the years, the building switched many hands and became many things. What was once a bank turned into a space that over time housed Roscoe’s School for Accounting, the International Order of Odd Fellows, as well as Baptist and Methodist church services.

In 1952, the original building was lost to a fire and plans were made to rebuild the space into a grand hall. Construction commenced and by 1854, the building was finally completed. Theater and musical performances were held here and became known as the Masonic Opera House. It wasn’t until 1915 that the building became known as the Majestic Theatre, and no more than two years later did the building experience its bleak involvement in one of the city’s most devastating events.

In 1918, Chillicothe’s military training base, Camp Sherman, was hit hard with the Spanish Influenza. Large amounts of people were dying every day and the camp had run out of space to take care of the dead. With this, many local buildings ended up being used as temporary morgues, including the Majestic Theatre.

“[The Majestic] actually became a temporary morgue. So in the dressing rooms downstairs…they would actually have bodies stacked like cordwood.” Says Gene Betts, a board member for the Majestic and an on and off volunteer since 2000. In another of Medert’s books titled “Chillicothe, Ohio and the Great War 1914-1918”, it’s mentioned that the theater’s seats “were removed to make room for the coffins”.

The stage of the theater was used for the embalming process. Part of this process includes draining the blood from the body and in this case, the blood was drained into what was known then as “Bank Alley”. After this tragic event, it would become known as “Blood Alley”.

“If you say ‘Blood Alley’ to people in Chillicothe, they still know you’re talking about the alley right beside the Majestic” says Betts.

Photo of "Blood Alley" the area right beside the Majestic Theatre where the blood from the dead was drained.

Along with the theater’s other reported paranormal instances, such as clapping from a supposedly empty balcony and the feeling of an unwelcoming presence in the dressing rooms, reports have been made of being able to see the ghost of a soldier lying on the stage. However, these reports and the building’s past haven’t deterred visitors. “I haven’t come across anybody that’s creeped out by it,” says Betts. “It is definitely an intrigue of the building, so I think it actually helps get awareness out about the Majestic, especially when we do our ghost walks…so we’ve embraced it.” The Ghost Walk is a fundraiser that’s held once a year and out of the 15 years it’s been carried out, the majestic has been has been featured at least 10 of the years.

Today, the Majestic Theatre is very active. They host many tribute band events as well as comedy nights, plays, and occasional movies and workshops. The grim hand the theater played in the city’s past doesn’t bother patrons and even attracts some of them. So the next time you’re in Chillicothe, stop by one of the Majestic’s performances to experience the history and the fun, and see if you can spot the paranormal.
