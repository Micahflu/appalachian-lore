Deep within the Zaleski State Forest, near the edge of Vinton County, Ohio, are the remnants of a community cemetery atop a hill, a few foundation stones scattered through the woods, and the 255 foot-long, brick lined tunnel that still bears the name of the mining community that once called the area home: Moonville.

Abandoned by its last remaining residents in 1947, Moonville was never a particularly large community—at its peak in the 1870s it had a population of about 100 people. It originated in 1856 along a strip of rail that ran through land belonging to Samuel Coe, who had struck a deal with the Marietta & Cincinnati Railroad that allowed them to freely build track through his land, and in exchange they would transport the clay and coal that could be found on his property. Moonville was one of several areas settled to take advantage of these mineral resources, however all of these settlements struggled with isolation due to their remote location, often being most easily accessed by railroad connecting them. This convenient shortcut, bypassing the hike through the hills and hollows, was a hazardous method of travel as the track crossed several sets of trestles spanning over ravines and creeks, as well as passing through the Moonville tunnel. Neither tunnel nor trestles were quite wide enough to allow safe passage while a train passed by, and were just a little too long to get out of the tunnel by the time you heard the train coming, which resulted in a number of tragic deaths that gave rise to one of Moonville’s most lasting legacies: its ghosts. Likely due to its captivating name and striking tunnel, it seems to have become the focal point for many of the incidents that happened even in the surrounding miles of track.

One of Moonville’s earliest ghosts was commonly sighted by the crews of freight trains in the 1890s, reportedly dressed in white robes and carrying a lantern (who’s come to be known as the Ghost of Moonville). The specter’s lantern has led some to speculate the ghost could be that of Frank Lawhead, an engineer who had been killed in 1880 along with the engine’s fireman, Charles Krick, in a head-on collision with another freight train. There are two other reports of fatal train wrecks around Moonville, an earlier one in 1874, another collision of freight trains resulting in the death of a fireman, and one in 1938 when a freight train ran into a rockslide west of the tunnel, killing the engineer. Train collisions weren’t the only danger to their crews, however, and oftentimes for those employed as brakeman the smallest mistake could be fatal (deaths reported in 1859, 1873, 1876, and 1884 of brakeman falling under the wheels of their own trains, or being crushed between cars).

Other notable stories involve the purported ghost of a woman whose appearance is often followed by a scent of lavender (there have been at least three recorded cases of women killed by trains around Moonville), and the ghost of David “Baldie” Keeton, a man who was potentially murdered but definitely run over by several trains before he was found the next morning in 1886, who is said to sit on top of the entrance to the Moonville tunnel and throw rocks at visitors walking into the tunnel.

Very little visibly remains of the houses, the old general store, the depot, or the post office that once comprised Moonville. Even the tracks, which were used up until the mid 1980’s, have been long since removed along with the bridges and trestles. A short walk from the tunnel, down the rail trail, across Raccoon Creek, and up the hill a ways you can still find the foundation stones of the old school house, and on the very top of the hill are the last remaining headstones of a cemetery that was said to have been “buried full” by a former resident of Moonville while being interviewed in 1977 by Rich Dahn, a historian associated with the Moonville Rail Trail Association. The Moonville Rail Trail Association is a non-profit organization that has been working since 2001 to convert the old rail line into a trail for public use. They maintain the tunnel and the trail and are working to replace the bridges to improve access to the trail, and in 2016 installed the Neil Shaw Memorial Bridge across Raccoon Creek.
