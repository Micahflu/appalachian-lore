If you ever find yourself near Adam’s County just north of Peebles, South East Ohio you’ll discover rich settler history and the mysterious Serpent Mound. The Serpent Mound has tons of prehistorical intrigue but it's also a visually beautiful earthen structure that draws thousands of people to visit it each year. The serpent mound is an official national historic landmark and the world’s largest serpent effigy in existence. National Geographic magazine refers to the mound as a “Great wonder of the Ancient World” and is referred to by Beth Jenkins, a representative from the Ohio historical society as “an Ohioan Gem."

Serpent mound is documented to be over 1,400 years old and is made from clay, ash, stones, and the soil and grass that cover it. The mound stretches to an astonishing 1,376 feet in total length just a little longer than 4 football fields. The massive like curves in the mound that give the mound its serpent like shape stretch about 800 feet. Beth mentions that there is a lot of mystery behind who constructed the mound. However, historians attribute the creation of the effigy to Adena culture. The Adena people were a group of native Americans that existed between 1000 to 200 B.C. and are known for their captivating man-made earthen structures that can still be seen today. In 1848 Serpent Mound was discovered by E. G. Squire and Edwin Davis, the fathers of modern-day archeology. These men wrote Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley a book that focuses on the study and discovery of prehistoric native and ingenious mounds and architecture.

The Serpent's open mouth is wrapped around a large oval; what the oval is meant to represent is up for interpretation - the most common beliefs are that it is an egg, the sun, or the Earth.

“Visitors feel such a connection to the site that they want to pay their respects with handmade offerings and gifts."

A bird's eye view of the head of Serpent Mound after a snow shower. The Serpent Mound remains closed to the public in the winter months, and reopens in March.

The mound is believed to be of astronomical significance to the Adena tribe. The entire body of the serpent from head to tail is serve a purpose in time keeping. The head of the serpent aligns with the sunset of the summer solstice while the tail aligns with the winter solstice. The three curves in the middle of the serpent’s body align with the summer solstice sunrise, the autumn equinox and spring equinox respectively. It’s wildly accepted among historical professionals that study ancient cultures and effigy's, that the mound was used by the Adena tribe to track the time of the year.

The Serpent & The Sun

The curves of the snake line up with the four major solar events - the summer and winter solstices, and the spring and fall equinoxes.

Serpent mound had still been used as farmland even after the landmark’s initial discovery in 1848. A barn and livestock sat atop the serpent structure with plowing damaging the surrounding area of the structure. Several years later in 1886 Fredrick Ward Putnam, an American anthropologist who had become enamored by the mound, had put efforts in place to preserve the and restore the mound. Putnam did so by buying several acres of the property and for a time the land was owned by Harvard University and then later gifted to the state of Ohio. In 1967 the Ohio historical society opened a Serpent Mound themed museum near the mound. The historical society had also constructed a path around the outside of the mound so that all who traveled to visit the structure would have an easier time walking along side it.

There are winding, narrow paths that follow the shape of the serpent around the Mound to enable visitors to get a good look at the serpent.

Serpent Mound is a global sensation that’s subject matter can be found in history books and classrooms around the world. The site is a representation of prehistoric manmade craftsmanship and the beauty of nature coming together as one perfectly to create a vision of awe for onlookers. Beth says that certain visitors feel a strong connection with the mound and will often walk along the path put in by the Ohio historical society, meditate in its presence, and often leave gifts and offerings for the mound in appreciation. The next time you’re in South eastern Ohio take the time to visit this magnificent structure that has withstood the tests of time and fascinated the world.
